The coaches winning with AI aren't writing essays to a chatbot — they run a handful of repeatable prompts that turn an hour of admin into a few minutes. The pattern every good one shares (and the thing the viral "just ask ChatGPT" posts skip): **specificity in, safety on.** Vague prompts give generic, sometimes wrong answers; the ones below are parameterized — fill the `[brackets]` with your client's real data and your own voice.

One rule sits over all of them, so read it first:

> **The guardrail.** Never let the model invent numbers or medical claims. Feed it *your* data, keep it inside a coach's scope, and make it flag anything that needs a professional. The prompt does the typing; your judgement signs it off.

### 1. The programming block — your biggest time sink

```
You are my assistant strength coach. Draft a [4]-week training block, then STOP
and ask me what to change before expanding it.
CLIENT: [age, training age, goal, days/week, session length, equipment, injuries].
RULES: progressive overload week to week, one deload, compound-first. List
sets × reps × RIR and rest. Do NOT invent the client's maxes — use the numbers I
give you: [recent lifts]. Flag anything that needs a professional's judgement.
```
*Why it works: the "stop and ask me first" line turns a wall of generic text into a draft you actually shape.*

### 2. The win-back — re-engage a client who went quiet

```
Write 5 short check-in messages (max 2 sentences each) for a client who's gone
quiet for [2] weeks. Supportive, specific, light accountability — never guilt.
Reference that their goal was [goal]. Give me 5 distinct angles so I can pick one.
```
*The version coaches share most: five options, not one, so it sounds like you — not a template.*

### 3. The myth-buster — content that actually travels

```
Write a punchy [LinkedIn/Instagram] post debunking ONE myth my [audience] believes
about [topic, e.g. "soreness means a good workout"]. Structure: hook → the myth →
why it's wrong in plain language → what to do instead → one-line CTA. Under [120]
words, my voice: [confident, no jargon]. Don't cite studies you can't name.
```
*Myth-busting is the single most-reshared format for wellness creators — one clear correction beats ten tips.*

### 4. Intake → first session, with red flags

```
Here are a new client's intake answers: [paste]. Turn them into: (1) a 45-minute
first-session plan, (2) the 3 questions I should ask in person, (3) any red flags
that mean I refer out or get medical clearance first. Be conservative on safety;
if something is outside a coach's scope, say so.
```
*This is where the guardrail earns its keep — you want the model surfacing risk, not glossing it.*

### 5. One session → a week of content (the creator's repurposing engine)

```
Here's a rough voice-note transcript of a coaching insight: [paste]. Turn it into a
week of content: 1 short-form video hook + script (<150 words), 3 tweet-threads, a
5-slide carousel outline, and 1 email subject + 2 lines. Every claim must stay
inside my transcript — add no stats. Match my tone: [ ].
```
*Record one good idea on your walk; ship a week of posts. Nothing invented, because the source is your own words.*

These five cover ~80% of the repetitive work — programming, retention, content, onboarding, distribution. You don't need a deck of 50; you need five you'll actually run every week, with your judgement on top.

Save them, adapt them to your voice, and build the five into your weekly workflow — the leverage is in running them consistently, not collecting more.
