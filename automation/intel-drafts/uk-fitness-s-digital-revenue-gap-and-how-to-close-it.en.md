## The Gap Nobody Is Talking About

UK fitness operators have spent years competing on class count, floor space, and monthly membership price. Meanwhile, the digital economy quietly built a different kind of fitness business — one measured not in bodies through the door but in revenue per online user. The data makes the contrast uncomfortable to ignore.

Broad eServices ARPU across the UK sits at $255.25 in 2024 and is forecast to climb toward $269.08 by 2027. Fitness eServices ARPU? $78.20 in 2024. That is not a niche segment underperforming slightly — that is a sector extracting less than a third of what the wider digital economy routinely earns from the same connected consumer. The fitness industry has built a digital presence without building digital pricing.

## Participation Volume Won't Save You

The instinct when revenue stalls is to chase more members. The Scotland participation data should temper that instinct hard. Adult sport participation has moved within a band of 9.41% to 9.81% across six years. The high and the low are separated by less than half a percentage point. This is not a market that suddenly opens up with a better Instagram campaign. The addressable pool in participation-constrained regions is structurally flat — operators who depend on volume growth as their primary engine are building on sand.

The implication is direct: yield per existing digital user must do the work that new-member acquisition cannot. That means rethinking what you charge for, not just how many people you charge.

## Your Window Is 2025–2027

The broader eServices ARPU forecast tells operators something tactically precise. The curve rises from $261.50 in 2025 to a peak of $269.08 in 2027, then softens. Consumer willingness to spend on digital services is not a permanent escalator — it has a shape, and that shape has a plateau. Fitness operators who delay building serious digital revenue infrastructure until 2028 or beyond are likely to find a market that has already recalibrated expectations downward.

This is the window. Not indefinitely — specifically now through 2027.

## What Operators Should Do

First, price your digital product against the broader digital economy, not just against other gyms. The fitness sector's ARPU baseline of $70.40 in 2017 has grown to $78.20 by 2024 — a slow crawl that reflects habitual underpricing, not an immovable ceiling.

Second, design for revenue per user rather than headcount. Bundle coaching, content, and accountability in ways that justify a meaningfully higher annual digital spend per member.

Third, move with urgency. The forecast plateau in broad eServices ARPU arrives by 2027. That is the deadline hiding inside this data.
