## The Quiet Squeeze Operators Aren't Seeing

Two numbers define the UK digital fitness opportunity right now: $78.2 and $269.08. The first is what the average online fitness user in the UK generates per year. The second is where the broader eServices market peaks in 2027. That $190+ gap is not just a curiosity — it is a structural under-monetisation problem, and closing even a fraction of it is the most actionable growth lever a UK fitness operator has in 2024.

## What the Data Actually Shows

Fitness eServices ARPU has risen every year since 2017, from $70.4 to $78.2 by 2024. That is consistent, which is good. But $7.80 of growth across seven years is also telling you something: the category has normalised at a low price point. Users are paying, but they are not paying much, and the market has not yet forced operators to justify more.

Zoom out to the broader eServices picture and the trajectory is sharper. ARPU climbs from $246.94 in 2023 to a projected $269.08 in 2027 — then it contracts. By 2028 it is already down to $264.06. That contraction is a maturity signal, not a catastrophe, but it sets a clock. The years between now and 2027 are when digital consumer willingness to pay is at its highest across the entire UK online economy. Fitness operators who are still underpriced in 2026 will have missed the window.

The participation data adds important context. In Scotland, the share of adults participating in sport barely moved between 2014 and 2019 — it ranged from 9.41% to 9.81%, a band of less than half a percentage point. This is not a growth market in the traditional sense. The people who exercise are exercising; the people who do not are not starting. For operators, that means the growth play is not about finding new participants — it is about extracting more value from the engaged audience that already exists.

## What Operators Should Do With This

Three moves follow directly from the data.

First, **reprice digital before 2027.** The eServices ARPU ceiling is visible and dated. Structured coaching programmes, accountability cohorts, and results-based memberships can command multiples of the current $78.2 benchmark — but only if operators make the case before the broader market softens.

Second, **invest in retention over acquisition.** A flat participation rate means the total addressable audience is not expanding organically. Every customer you keep is more valuable than in a growing market because replacements are harder to find.

Third, **build hybrid offers for stable markets.** Regions with consistent but non-growing participation — the pattern Scotland's data illustrates — are ideal for hybrid models that convert existing in-person clients into digital subscribers, layering revenue without requiring new demand.

The data is not alarming. But it is directional. The window is open; it will not stay that way.
